Team,

We've received an RFP from Wyoming Game & Fish, the state agency responsible for managing grey wolf populations in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem. Grey wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone in 1995 and their population has experienced significant volatility since, shaped by multiple rounds of federal listing and delisting under the Endangered Species Act. A parvovirus and mange outbreak in 2026 has reduced the Greater Yellowstone wolf population to its lowest recorded level since the reintroduction era, creating simultaneous uncertainty about long-term population viability and continued political pressure from ranching communities over livestock losses.

At peak depredation, documented wolf-killed cattle incidents reached 121 animals in a single year across the state. The Defenders of Wildlife compensation fund has paid over $1.4 million in verified rancher compensation over the programme's life. Non-lethal deterrence options are available: livestock guardian dogs cost $1,000–3,000 per animal, and range rider programmes typically run $50,000–80,000 per rider per year. The agency notes that non-lethal deterrent adoption is voluntary and spatially uneven, concentrated on operations that have previously participated in cost-share programmes.

They want us to develop a comprehensive strategy to:
1. Keep annual confirmed livestock depredation incidents below 40 cattle-equivalent animals statewide
2. Maintain a population sufficient to meet federal minimum viability requirements and prevent ESA relisting
3. Deliver a management framework defensible under both state wildlife law and the current federal delisting agreement

They have provided programme data including:
- Annual pack count, territory, and depredation incident data by zone (2010–2026)
- Confirmed and probable livestock kill records with compensation payment history
- Non-lethal deterrent adoption rates and documented effectiveness by operation type
- ESA relisting threshold criteria from USFWS correspondence
- Budget available for both compensation and preventive measures

The agency faces a narrow operational window: the current federal delisting status permits lethal removal of confirmed livestock-killing wolves under state authority, but any significant population decline risks triggering federal relisting and removal of that authority. A management plan must be filed with the state legislature before the next session.

Please provide your initial strategic recommendations within 72 hours.

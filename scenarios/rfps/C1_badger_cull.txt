Team,

We've received an RFP from the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA), the operational arm of the Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs responsible for managing bovine tuberculosis (bTB) in England. bTB is the most expensive endemic livestock disease in the UK, costing the government over £100 million annually in testing, compensation, and movement restrictions, and new herd breakdown rates in high-risk areas remain above statutory targets despite a decade of active intervention. APHA's programme data shows that the licensed culling programme, running since 2013, has removed 247,880 badgers under licence at an operational cost of approximately £6,785 per animal removed. Free-shooting accounts for 87.7% of removals.

Their modelling projects a 9–16% reduction in new herd breakdowns in zones where culling is sustained over nine years. The agency notes that Wales has achieved a comparable bTB decline over the same period using only enhanced cattle movement controls and testing frequency, without operating a culling programme, and at substantially lower per-area cost.

They want us to develop a comprehensive strategy to:
1. Reduce new bTB herd breakdown incidence by 20% within five years across the current high-risk area
2. Achieve this at minimum whole-programme cost per herd breakdown prevented
3. Produce a defensible evidence base for parliamentary review within the six-month Secretary of State announcement window

They have provided programme data including:
- Annual removal counts and costs by zone and method (2013–2024)
- New herd breakdown rates pre- and post-cull commencement by county
- Wales bTB trend data (enhanced cattle testing only, no culling)
- Badger vaccination pilot results from Cornwall and Gloucestershire
- Budget ceiling of £45M for the five-year period

The agency faces active legal challenge from animal welfare organisations on licence renewal and sustained pressure from agricultural unions for faster measurable progress. A full strategic review has been authorised, and APHA is prepared to consider modifications to the existing programme design.

This engagement is politically time-sensitive: the Secretary of State is due to announce the next phase of the national bTB eradication strategy within six months.

Please provide your initial strategic recommendations within 72 hours.

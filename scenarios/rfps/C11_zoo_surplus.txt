We've received an RFP from the European Association of Zoos and Aquaria (EAZA), the organisation that coordinates captive breeding programmes across more than 400 member institutions. EAZA manages a studbook-based genetic programme for the reticulated giraffe, coordinating breeding recommendations to maximise genetic diversity and population viability across participating zoos. The programme currently faces a recurring surplus challenge: breeding success has exceeded the network's placement capacity, creating a population of animals whose genetic lines are already overrepresented in the studbook and for which no suitable placement exists within the programme.

This challenge was brought into sharp public focus in February 2014, when Copenhagen Zoo euthanised Marius, a healthy 18-month-old male giraffe, on the grounds that his lineage — represented by 38 offspring, 20 of which had already been distributed to European zoos — was overrepresented in the studbook and that no suitable placement existed within the programme. EAZA's governing policy explicitly permits lethal management of surplus animals where it results in a "quick death without suffering," a position the association defends on genetic programme integrity grounds. Copenhagen Zoo does not use hormonal contraception or sterilisation, on the basis that parenthood and natural reproductive behaviour contribute to animal welfare. Transfer offers from North American institutions were declined on programme integrity grounds. This case is now the reference point for public and regulatory scrutiny of EAZA surplus management practices across all species.

They want us to develop a comprehensive strategy to:
1. Manage current and projected giraffe studbook surplus while maintaining programme genetic diversity targets
2. Establish a surplus management policy that can withstand public and regulatory scrutiny across EAZA member jurisdictions
3. Reduce institutional inconsistency in how surplus management decisions are made and communicated across member zoos

They have provided programme data including:
- Studbook genetic diversity indices, breeding recommendations, and current surplus classifications
- Member zoo capacity and placement availability data by region and species
- Contraception and sterilisation trial data from EAZA primate and ungulate programmes
- Media incident records related to surplus management across member zoos (2010–2024)
- Regulatory variation in permitted surplus management methods across member countries

EAZA is operating under increasing regulatory attention in multiple member jurisdictions, including the UK and Germany, where legislative proposals would restrict lethal surplus management of healthy animals. A revised policy framework must be adopted at the next general assembly in eight months.

Please provide your initial strategic recommendations within 72 hours.
